# Metallicity of the Large Magellanic Cloud: A Synthesis of Recent Observations

## Overview of LMC Metallicity

The mean metallicity of the Large Magellanic Cloud (LMC) field star population has been determined from near-infrared VISTA Survey of the Magellanic Clouds (VMC) data to be [Fe/H] = −0.42 dex with a dispersion of σ [Fe/H] = 0.04 dex. This result was obtained from high-spatial-resolution metallicity maps covering approximately 105 deg² across the LMC using near-infrared passbands, extending out to a radius of roughly 6 kpc. [S1], [S16]

## Metallicity Distribution and Spatial Variations

The metallicity distribution derived from VMC data shows a notably narrower dispersion compared with optical surveys. The VMC metallicity distribution has σ [Fe/H] = 0.04 dex, which is much smaller than the distributions obtained from the Magellanic Cloud Photometric Survey (MCPS) and OGLE III data, which have dispersions of σ [Fe/H] = 0.10–0.11 dex for all three specified regions in the LMC. [S2]

When comparing the mean metallicities from different surveys, the VMC estimate of [Fe/H] = −0.42 dex is relatively metal-poor compared with MCPS ([Fe/H] = −0.37 dex) and OGLE III ([Fe/H] = −0.39 dex). The range of metallicities estimated using VMC data is smaller compared with those obtained using MCPS and OGLE III data. [S17] The metallicity in the NIR maps ranges from approximately −0.22 dex to −0.60 dex, whereas the previous optical maps showed a wider variation from −0.10 dex to −0.90 dex. [S20]

### Regional Metallicity Differences

The bar region of the LMC is found to be mildly metal-rich compared with the outer disc, showing evidence of a shallow gradient in metallicity from the galaxy's centre to a radius of 6 kpc. [S1] The most metal-rich points (greater than −0.31 dex) are located in the bar, with only a few metal-poor points (greater than −0.53 dex) scattered in the outskirts. Metal-poor regions with metallicities below −0.55 dex are located in the outer disc at radii greater than 2°. [S12]

The northern disc of the LMC has a larger range in metallicity compared with the southern part, a trend similar to what was observed in the MCPS maps. The eastern and western disc of the LMC have a similar range in metallicity, a trend also observed in the OGLE III maps. [S20]

## The Metallicity Gradient

### Quantified Gradient Values

Analysis of the radial metallicity gradient from the VMC data indicates a shallow gradient of approximately −0.008 ± 0.001 dex kpc−1 from the galaxy's centre to a radius of 6 kpc. [S5], [S16] This gradient is described as negligible when compared with previous estimates. [S16]

Analysis of individual field age-metallicity relationships from wide-field Washington photometry found coefficients suggesting a metallicity gradient of approximately (−0.01 ± 0.01) dex kpc−1 when adopting an LMC distance of 50 kpc. [S3] The coefficients from the fitted expression [Fe/H] = C + (∂[Fe/H]/∂t) × t + (∂[Fe/H]/∂a) × a were C = −0.55 ± 0.02 dex, ∂[Fe/H]/∂t = −0.047 ± 0.003 dex Gyr⁻¹, and ∂[Fe/H]/∂a = −0.007 ± 0.006 dex deg⁻¹. [S3]

### Evidence for Gradient Absence

Despite some evidence of a gradient, other analyses find no significant metallicity gradient in the LMC. This result agrees with the findings of Grocholski et al. (2006) who found that the LMC lacks any metallicity gradient. The relatively more metal-poor stars found in the outermost regions are mostly a consequence of the fact that such regions are dominated by old stars which are relatively metal-poor, whereas intermediate-age stars which are more metal-rich prevail in the innermost regions. [S3]

### Asymmetric Gradient Features

The LMC's radial metallicity gradient is asymmetric. It is metal-poor and flatter towards the southwest, in the direction of the Magellanic Bridge. This hints at mixing and/or distortion of the spatial metallicity distribution, presumably caused by tidal interactions between the Magellanic Clouds. [S5] A similar asymmetry was previously identified in the Small Magellanic Cloud, where radial metallicity gradients appeared flatter towards the LMC, potentially indicating stellar mixing caused by LMC–SMC interactions. [S6]

## Age-Metallicity Relationship

### Field Star Population

Analysis of approximately 5.5 million stars in twenty-one 36′ × 36′ fields distributed throughout the LMC main body, subdivided into 336 smaller 9′ × 9′ subfields, has provided insights into the age-metallicity relationship. The individual field age-metallicity relationships show noticeable differences from field to field in several aspects: starting and ending ages, metallicity range, shape, and other characteristics. [S14]

The composite age-metallicity relationship for the LMC fields reveals that, while old and metal-poor field stars have been preferentially formed in the outer disk, younger and more metal-rich stars prevail in the innermost regions. [S14] The most distant fields have been preferentially formed at a low and relatively constant metallicity level from the birth of the LMC until approximately 6 Gyr ago, while the inner fields have been formed later on with a steeper chemical enrichment rate. [S7]

### Metallicity Range Variations

The range in the metallicity of the LMC fields has varied during the lifetime of the LMC. A small range of metal abundance is found for the outer disk fields, whereas an average range of Δ[Fe/H] = +0.3 ± 0.1 dex is found in the inner disk fields. [S9] The metallicity error bars cover a larger range than the points represent because they include both the standard dispersion of the points and their measured errors. [S7]

### Chemical Evolution Models

Comparison of the composite age-metallicity relationship with theoretical models suggests that the LMC has not chemically evolved as a closed-box or bursting system exclusively, but as a combination of both scenarios that have had different prominence during the lifetime of the galaxy, with the bursting model generally more dominant. Enhanced formation episodes could have possibly taken place as a result of interactions with the Milky Way and/or the Small Magellanic Cloud. [S9]

## Comparison with Cluster Data

A compilation of age and metallicity measurements of 85 star clusters in the LMC from the peer-reviewed literature has been used for comparison with field star results. The cluster ages are derived from isochrone fitting in colour–magnitude diagram planes, and the metallicities are derived from spectroscopy or isochrone fitting. [S11] The essential features of the age-metallicity relationship have not changed when comparing field star results with cluster data. [S18]

When comparing composite field age-metallicity relationships with cluster age-metallicity relationships, there are differences in the chemical evolution traced by field stars and star clusters. The oldest Small Magellanic Cloud cluster is at the young and metal-rich extreme of the LMC globular cluster distributions. [S14]

## Methodological Considerations

### Technique Used for VMC Maps

The metallicity maps were derived by identifying red giant branch stars in spatially distinct Y, (Y − Ks) colour–magnitude diagrams. In any of the selected subregions, the RGB slope is used as an indicator of the average metallicity, based on calibration to metallicity using spectroscopic data. [S1] Approximately 4663 subregions were analyzed within a radius of 6 kpc. [S16]

### Comparison Between Surveys

For subregions at radii from 0 to 1 kpc, the metallicity ranges differ between surveys: MCPS shows −0.250 to −0.30 dex, OGLE III shows −0.325 to −0.375 dex, and VMC shows −0.375 to −0.425 dex. For subregions at radii from 4 to 5 kpc, the ranges are −0.40 to −0.45 dex for MCPS, −0.50 to −0.55 for OGLE III, and −0.40 to −0.45 dex for VMC. [S10] These differences arise because the depth and resolution of MCPS and OGLE III data are different, and different area binning criteria were used for both data sets. [S10]
