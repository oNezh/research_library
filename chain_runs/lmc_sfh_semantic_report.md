# The Star Formation History of the Large Magellanic Cloud

## Overview and Significance

The Large Magellanic Cloud (LMC) represents one of the most important targets for understanding stellar populations and galactic evolution due to its close proximity, favorable viewing angle, and ongoing star formation activity [S1]. The foundational work of Hodge (1960, 1961) established that while 35 LMC clusters are similar in age to Milky Way globular clusters, the LMC contains 23 populous clusters that are clearly much younger than any Milky Way globular cluster, suggesting that the star formation history (SFH) of the LMC might be fundamentally different from that of the Milky Way [S1].

Modern analysis has established that the LMC's SFH differs dramatically from that of the Milky Way, with a distinctive pattern of formation episodes that has been documented through both cluster studies and field star population analysis [S1]. Age-dating of LMC clusters via isochrone fitting to color–magnitude diagrams (CMDs) began with the CCD era, and by 1989 over 100 LMC clusters had been analyzed [S12]. The age–metallicity relation (AMR) of the LMC's star clusters contains perplexing features, such as the lack of clusters between 5 and 12 Gyr old, and a wide metallicity dispersion among young (t < 3 Gyr) clusters [S12].

## The Global Star Formation History

Harris & Zaritsky (2009) presented the first-ever global, spatially resolved reconstruction of the SFH of the LMC, based on the application of StarFISH analysis software to multiband photometry of 20 million stars from the Magellanic Clouds Photometric Survey [S2]. Following an initial burst of star formation, there was a quiescent epoch from approximately 12 to 5 Gyr ago [S2]. Star formation then resumed and has proceeded until the current time at an average rate of roughly 0.2 M⊙ yr⁻¹, with temporal variations [S2].

The global integrated SFH reveals that half of the LMC's stellar mass was assembled approximately 5 Gyr ago [S9]. A significant portion of the LMC's stellar mass had been assembled by 10 Gyr ago [S8]. The cumulative stellar mass formed includes the initial mass of all stars formed, including stars that have already evolved to non-luminous end-states and returned large fractions of their mass to the interstellar medium [S9].

## The Quiescent Epoch and Its Implications

During the long quiescent epoch that followed the initial star formation, the LMC all but ceased forming stars, sustaining an average SFR of only 0.02 M⊙ yr⁻¹ over the interval between 12 and 5 Gyr [S4]. This quiescent epoch is also present in the total SFH derived from solutions that were not tied to HST results, confirming that it is not an artifact of the adopted HST solutions, but a robust feature of the LMC's history reflected in both its cluster and field populations [S4].

The question of what caused the general lull in star formation between approximately 5 and 12 Gyr ago remains a significant unanswered question [S3]. Piatti & Geisler (2013) noted that there existed a long relatively quiescent epoch during which the star formation was suppressed throughout the LMC, and the metallicity also remained stagnant during this period [S3].

The early history was characterized by rapid enrichment, reaching [Fe/H] = −1.2 by about 12 Gyr ago [S7]. The metallicity then remained stagnant throughout the quiescent epoch [S7]. This chemical enrichment history is consistent with the cluster age–metallicity relation [S7].

## The Resumption of Star Formation

About 5 Gyr ago, star formation resumed throughout the LMC and has been ongoing since [S7]. This global resumption of star formation is strongly suggestive of a dramatic change in the LMC, such as the merger of a gas-rich dwarf galaxy or a particularly dramatic tidal encounter, probably with the Small Magellanic Cloud (SMC) given the similar resumption of star formation seen in that galaxy's SFH [S7].

Recent work by Mazzi et al. (2021) using VMC data over an area of 96 deg² (twice larger than Harris & Zaritsky 2009) found a similar SFH but with a lower star-formation rate at young ages [S11]. Their maps show that the SFR at ages younger than 63 Myr is patchy and centralized, while at older ages (< 1.6 Gyr), the SFR extends to larger radii [S11]. The period of enhanced star formation in the LMC occurs approximately between 500 Myr and 4 Gyr, with two peaks of enhanced SFR in the age intervals 630 Myr to 1 Gyr and 1.6–4 Gyr [S11].

## Hubble Space Telescope Constraints

Despite their exquisite depth, HST-based SFH solutions are necessarily incomplete because the HST field of view is vanishingly small compared to the solid angle subtended by the LMC—a full-coverage survey with ACS would require roughly 50,000 pointings [S15]. However, among HST studies targeting the bar, there is remarkable consensus regarding the general outlines of the LMC's early SFH [S5].

Three independent studies using deep HST imaging of LMC bar fields—Olsen (1999), Holtzman et al. (1999), and Smecker-Hane et al. (2002)—agree remarkably well [S6]. Each finds an initial epoch of star formation that is extinguished by 10 Gyr ago, and then resumes about 5 Gyr ago [S6]. The shape of the Olsen solution is somewhat different than the other two, but this disagreement is minor compared to the overall agreement [S6].

## Spatial Variation in the Star Formation History

The LMC's SFH has rich structure both spatially and temporally [S8]. Due to dynamical mixing and the longer time intervals covered, the older time bins are generally spatially and temporally smoother than the younger bins [S8]. In the youngest bins, peaks in the map can be associated with well-known star-forming regions such as 30 Doradus and LMC Constellation III [S8].

### The LMC Bar

The LMC bar shows intermittent periods of activity and quiescence throughout the LMC's history [S14]. It had particularly active episodes at 5 Gyr, 500 Myr, and 100 Myr ago [S14]. The biggest distinguishing features of the bar's SFH are that its quiescent periods at 250 Myr and 1 Gyr are relatively deeper than in other parts of the LMC, and the peak in the SFR at 5 Gyr is relatively stronger [S17]. The SFH of the LMC bar is qualitatively similar to that of non-bar regions, indicating that the stars in the bar have likely always been part of the LMC [S10].

In the SFH map, the bar shows intermittent periods of activity and quiescence throughout the LMC's history, as noted previously by Hardy et al. (1984) and Smecker-Hane et al. (2002) [S14]. There is striking coherence in the timing of star formation events among the bar, arm, and void regions [S17]. Whatever its nature, the bar has been an integral part of the LMC for most of its history [S17].

### The LMC Arms

The LMC's "arms" refer to the structures extending off the ends of the LMC bar that are morphologically similar to spiral arms, without implying that there is necessarily a spiral density wave in the LMC's disk [S17]. The integrated SFHs of the northwest and southeast LMC arm regions are remarkably different [S17].

The northwest arm region had very little star formation activity prior to 5 Gyr ago, and it also lacks the quiescent period at 1 Gyr seen in most other regions [S14]. The activity at 100 Myr seen in most other regions also seems to be suppressed in the northwest arm, and its recent SFH is characterized by a steady increase in the SFR since 100 Myr ago [S14]. In the southeast arm, one of the strongest signatures of the short-term burst of activity at 2.5 Gyr is observed [S14].

### The Northwest Void

The northwest void is a region of the LMC's disk that shows no enhanced star formation activity at any epoch [S19]. However, the integrated SFH of the northwest void closely resembles the total integrated SFH of the entire LMC [S19]. In this sense, the void offers a look at the LMC's typical stellar population, unpolluted by recent localized deviations from the average behavior [S19].

## Interaction History and Common History with the SMC

There are several temporally coincident features in the global SFH solutions of the LMC and SMC [S10]. Both galaxies experienced a long quiescent epoch starting roughly 10 Gyr ago, and ending 5 Gyr ago in the LMC, and 3 Gyr ago in the SMC [S10]. Both galaxies experienced a significant peak in the SFR 2–3 Gyr ago [S10]. Both galaxies also show enhanced star formation activity around 400 Myr ago [S10]. These correlations suggest a common history for the Magellanic Clouds, stretching back over at least the past few Gyr [S10].

In the LMC, the stars that were formed during the 2–3 Gyr peak are now centrally concentrated in the LMC, although they do not seem to conform to the contours of the LMC bar [S10]. In the SMC, the stars formed at this time are not centrally concentrated today; indeed, they seem to be distributed in a ring-like structure [S10].

Harris & Zaritsky (2004) and Zaritsky & Harris (2004) noted coincidences between peaks in the SMC's SFR and past perigalactic interactions with the LMC and Milky Way [S19]. The most striking coincident peak occurred 2.5 Gyr ago, coinciding with what was previously identified as the time of a triple-interaction among the LMC, SMC, and Milky Way [S19].

## Age-Metallicity Relationship and Chemical Evolution

The chemical enrichment history inferred from the SFH analysis is consistent with the cluster age–metallicity relation [S7]. The early history was characterized by rapid enrichment, reaching [Fe/H] = −1.2 by about 12 Gyr ago, followed by stagnation throughout the quiescent epoch [S7].

Piatti & Geisler (2013) found that their age-metallicity relationship (AMR) suggests no significant chemical evolution from about 12 to 6 Gyr, even though stars were formed [S18]. The closed-box models appear to be qualitatively closer to Harris & Zaritsky (2009)'s reconstructed AMR [S18]. Their composite AMR has several important advantages over previous ones and may reconcile previous conclusions about the major enrichment processes that have dominated the chemical evolution of the LMC from its birth until approximately 1 Gyr ago [S18].

The LMC appears to show outside-in evolution of the disk, as opposed to the ΛCDM prediction for inside-out formation [S16]. The inner fields appeared to start their strong star formation episodes at about the same time that the outer fields were undergoing their last episode, which is not what would be expected if the impulse driving the onset of star formation were some global effect like a close galactic encounter [S16].

## Evidence for Field-Cluster Coupling

The field and cluster star formation modes appear to have been tightly coupled throughout the LMC's history [S1]. Harris & Zaritsky (2009) concluded that the field and cluster star formation modes have been tightly coupled throughout the LMC's history [S3].

However, Piatti & Geisler (2013) found that there exists a noticeable number of fields with representative ages spanning the age gap (from approximately 12 Gyr to 3 Gyr), which strengthens the difference between cluster and field star formation during this epoch [S18]. This suggests that the relationship between cluster and field star formation may be more complex than previously thought [S18].

## Recent Star Formation Activity

The spatial correlation of young (Age < 12.5 Myr) star formation activity derived from the analysis and Hα emission in the LMC from the Magellanic Clouds Emission Line Survey (MCELS) shows that Hα flux and recent star formation activity correlate as expected [S8]. The SFR at ages younger than 63 Myr is patchy and centralized [S11].

Studies from the 1970s found that Cepheids with periods in the range of 0.9, with ages in the range of (4–5) × 10⁷ years, are very strongly concentrated near the center of the LMC Bar [S13]. The conclusion that the Bar was an active center of star formation until fairly recently was confirmed by Tifft & Snell (1971), who found that the color-magnitude diagram of the Bar contains a narrow vertical blue sequence of relatively young stars [S13]. The remarkable shift from a concentration of star formation in the Bar to star formation predominantly outside the Bar that took place only 4 × 10⁷ years ago remains entirely unexplained [S13].

## Future Directions

While the collected SFH solutions based on HST data are necessarily incomplete, the deep HST imaging should sample the old SFH fairly because orbital mixing will have erased any spatial variation [S6]. Until recently, the Magellanic Clouds were widely regarded as long-term satellite galaxies of the Milky Way, in decaying orbits that would eventually lead to their consumption by our Galaxy [S19]. Future studies with more complete spatial coverage and deeper photometry will help to resolve remaining questions about the LMC's star formation history, including what caused the quiescent epoch and the nature of the 5 Gyr ago event that triggered the resumption of star formation.
